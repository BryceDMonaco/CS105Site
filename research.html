"A Novel Data-Compression Technique for Faster Computer Programs"

A PDF version of this article can be found here.

This article presented a new data-compression technique developed by researchers at MIT which works by compressing objects in memory. When compared to traditional cache-based compression methods, it compressed nearly twice as much and reduced memory usage by 50%. This is useful in any computer system because it significantly increases the amount of free memory available. I think this will affect me because I use computer systems in almost every aspect of my life every day. I think this will affect me by speeding up my work on computers and allowing me to run multiple programs or to run single programs faster, because they can run longer before needing to wait for memory to free up. I can prepare myself for the impact of this issue by adopting technologies which utilize this new technique and by potentially developing with this technique in mind to help it spread.

"Microsoft Confirms You Really, Really Don’t Need to ‘Safely Remove’ USB Flash Drives Anymore"

A PDF version of this article can be found here.

This article discusses a new feature released in the latest Windows 10 version which results in users no longer needing to worry about ejecting their flash drives or othe USB storage devices before they physically unplug them from the computer. It is done by disabling a cache technique which is used to speed up file transfers, but has the added drawback of needing to eject the flash drive (to make sure their is no data left in the cache, which would cause corruption). I think this will affect me slightly because I still occasionally use USB flash drives to transfer data between machines when an over-the-air or over the internet method is not available. I expect this to affect me by slightly reducing file transfer speeds, but by also speeding up my workflow because I no longer need to worry about going through the process of ejecting a device. I can embrace this by continuing to use my flash drives when I need them and by updating to the latest version of Windows 10.

"Facebook Stored Millions of Instagram Passwords in Plain Text"

A PDF version of this article can be found here.

This article discusses Facebook admitting to storing a significant amount of user passwords in plain text, this means the passwords were not encrypted and could be simply viewed by anyone. It originally admitted to only tens of thousands of accounts being affected, but later corrected that to millions of accounts, which is incredibly alarming. This is another example of companies simply not caring about the security of their users and instead opting to go the lazy and cheap way and simply store passwords as normal text. I think this will affect me because I have an Instagram account, and it has a password which I use on some accounts on other websites. I think this will affect me by people potentially seeing my password at the time of the breach. I can prepare myself for the impact of this issue by changing my password for Instagram and changing my password for other accounts which use the same username, email, and/or password.

"Will Virtual Reality Transform Real-World Laws?"

A PDF version of this article can be found here.

This article discusses potential legal impacts of virtual reality (VR) becoming increasingly popular. I think the impacts discussed by this article will affect me because I develop for VR and use it recreationally to play a few games. VR is a technology that is new and exciting, and I do my best to keep up with it. At least for the next few years, I do not expect a major impact on me or others, simply because VR is still treated as a "wild west" of sorts where everyone is constantly experimenting. The article discusses potential legal ramifications/punishments for something such as punching another player in VR. I think that this idea is absurd, if a user puts on some sort of device/equipment that allows them to feel pain from VR, they are likely aware of the potential for pain and, by using such a device, likely agree to not hold anyone liable for harm. I think the only real point the author of this article brings up is the use of trademarked material for characters in-game. This is a legal impact that exists right now and can simply be handled as any other trademark violation would. I can prepare myself for the impact of this issue by paying attention to assets I use in any sort of development for VR, being sure to avoid or get a licsense to use trademarked material, additionally I can take the time to be aware of potential risks I will take when I interact in VR.

"Putting Data Privacy in the Hands of Users"

A PDF version of this article can be found here.

This article discusses a solution to combat against the increasing loss of indiividual privacy in today's online world. With data leaks becoming very common, it is the users', and therefore my, best interest to find solutions to make up for loss of privacy. The solution proposed is a system called "Riverbed" in which users can specify their own rules for how their data is used, and servers are required to follow those rules. A tool such as this has a very high positive impact on me and could change how I browse the internet. I am always concerned with how my data is handled by companies and having the ability to explicitly control how it is used is something I would very much like. As a computer science student, I can prepare for this by supporting this platform or similar platforms. My system using this platform creates trust between me and my users and shows that I am working in their best interests, not mine. As a consumer, I can prepare for this by using it in my normal online activities. By using this tool, it encourages development and support for it, increasing my privacy online.

This article presents a new device which can convert wifi signals into DC voltage. The goal of this device, and devices like this, is to eventually replace batteries in low power devices, especially medical devices, to mititgate battery-related risks. I think this will affect me by expanding the possibilities for wireless power for devices. Currently, the device shown in the article is meant for small devices, but the authors propose a future where we have smart devices which power themselves just off of wifi signals, which are everywhere in our modern world. I think this will contribute to ending needing to charge battery-powered devices, instead, simply being somewhere where wifi signals are found will keep my devices charged. I can prepare for this by continuing to use wifi, therefore increasing the ubiquity of wifi signals.
